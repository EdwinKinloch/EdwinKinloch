What Fifteen Years of Infrastructure Prepared Me For

When I started experimenting with letting AI agents browse the web and act on what they found, I kept having a specific feeling: I've done this before. Not literally — but the shape of the problem was familiar.

An agent that reads untrusted content and can take actions is, structurally, the same problem as a network boundary that receives untrusted traffic and can be tricked into doing something it shouldn't. You don't trust input by default. You delimit it, you validate it, you assume it's hostile until proven otherwise, and you build a boundary that fails closed instead of open.

Most of what's called 'AI security' right now is the same discipline security and infrastructure people have practiced for two decades, wearing a new name. Least privilege still means least privilege. Defense in depth still means defense in depth. The tooling changed; the judgment didn't.

That's the case, as far as I'm concerned, for why infrastructure people age well into this field instead of out of it. The specific technology keeps turning over. The instinct for what a system will do under pressure — and what it will do when someone's actively trying to break it — is the part that compounds.
